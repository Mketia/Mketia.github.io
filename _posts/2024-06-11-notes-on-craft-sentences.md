Good sentences are not decorated thoughts. They are thoughts — the thinking and the expression are inseparable. A sentence that is hard to parse is usually a thought that hasn't been fully formed.

A few things I keep coming back to when working on sentences.

## Length and breath

A sentence has a duration. Short sentences land hard. Longer ones can hold complexity, sustain a mood, carry the reader through subordinate clauses without losing them — but only if the structure is right and the reward at the end justifies the journey.

The mistake is uniformity. All short sentences produce a staccato rhythm that exhausts the reader. All long ones produce fog. Variation is what makes prose feel alive.

Read your work aloud. Where you run out of breath, the sentence is too long or the structure has gone wrong. Where you stop too abruptly, you've cut something the reader still needed.

## The first word and the last

The positions of power in a sentence are the first and last words. The last carries the most weight — it's what lingers after the sentence ends. The first sets the frame.

Weak sentences often bury what matters in the middle. The fix is to restructure so that the thing you most want the reader to feel or remember is at the end.

> "In the beginning was the Word." Not: "The Word was what was in the beginning."

The difference is not just rhythm. It's emphasis. One version lands. The other trails off.

## Cutting is the work

Most first-draft sentences are longer than they need to be. This is not a failure — it's how thinking works. We write around the thing before we write the thing.

The revision question I ask most often is: *what is this sentence actually trying to say?* Write that down in as few words as possible. Now compare it to what you have. Usually the shorter version is better. Sometimes it's missing something the original had. But you've clarified what you're working with.

Every word should be doing something. Adverbs are often a sign that the verb is not strong enough. Adjectives often signal that the noun was a placeholder. The more specific the noun, the fewer adjectives it needs.

---

Sentences are where the real work happens. Essays, books, and arguments are made of them. If the sentences are good, almost everything else follows.
