A personal website is one of the few places on the internet that you actually own. Not rent. Own. No algorithm decides what order your posts appear in. No platform can deactivate your account for an unexplained policy violation. No company can change its business model and make your years of work inaccessible overnight.

This has happened, repeatedly, to writers who built their audience on platforms they didn't control. It will keep happening.

## Ownership and permanence

The case for a personal website is partly a case for ownership. The words you write at your own domain will be there as long as you keep the lights on. You decide the structure, the design, what stays up and what comes down.

This sounds simple, but it's increasingly rare. Most writing now happens inside systems that benefit from your writing but don't belong to you.

There's something worth preserving about the early web's implicit assumption: that if you made something, it was yours.

## Discovery still matters

I know the objection: no one will find your personal site. The platforms have the eyeballs. If you want to reach people, that's where you have to be.

This is partially true, and I don't think the answer is to abandon platforms entirely. Cross-post. Link back. Use social media as a signpost, not a destination.

But discovery is overrated as the primary metric for writing. If you write only for reach, you'll write for the algorithm. The algorithm is bad at appreciating nuance, ambiguity, length, or ideas that require context. The personal website is where you can afford to write for the reader who actually shows up — the one who typed your URL deliberately, or followed a link from someone who trusted you.

That reader is more valuable than ten thousand passive scrollers.

## The long game

I've had this site in various forms for years. Some of what's on it, I'd write differently now. Some of it I'm proud of. All of it is mine.

There's an archive here. Dated entries, old thinking, a record of something. In a culture that prefers the newest thing, there's quiet satisfaction in something that accumulates rather than disappears.

Start a website. Keep it simple. Write on it when you have something to say. Update it slowly and honestly. Let it be the place you direct people when someone asks: *where can I read your work?*
