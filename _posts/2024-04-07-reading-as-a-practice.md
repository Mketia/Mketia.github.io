There's a genre of reading advice that treats books as raw material for productivity. You read to extract insights, capture notes, build a knowledge system. The book is a mine; you are the miner.

I've tried this. It works, in a sense. I do retain more when I take notes. The synthesized fragments end up in my writing. The system compounds over time.

But I want to defend something else: reading that doesn't immediately produce anything. Reading for its own sake, with no output in mind, no notes, no synthesis, no summary.

## The case for uncommitted reading

When I read with a highlighter in one hand and a notebook nearby, I read differently. I'm scanning for the extractable. I'm asking: *is this quotable? Does this connect to something I already know?*

That's useful. But it's not the only way to read, and I think it's crowding out something valuable.

Reading without intention — following the book wherever it goes, losing yourself in an argument, re-reading a paragraph because it was beautiful, not because it was useful — this is a different kind of attention. It's slower and harder to quantify, but I think it does something that the extractive approach can't do.

It lets the reading change you without you directing the change.

## What I mean by that

The books that have most influenced how I think are not the ones I highlighted most carefully. They're the ones I was surprised by. The argument I didn't expect. The book I read in the wrong order, or too young, or at exactly the right moment for reasons I couldn't have predicted.

You can't optimize for surprise. You can only stay open to it.

There's a kind of intelligence that develops only through prolonged exposure to how other minds work — the rhythms of thought, the shapes of arguments, the ways different writers approach the same problems. You don't acquire it by taking notes. You acquire it by reading, and reading, and reading.

## A practical thought

I keep two reading tracks. One is deliberate: books connected to current work or problems, reading actively, taking notes. The other is uncommitted: whatever I feel like, read as slowly or quickly as I want, nothing required of it except that I show up.

The uncommitted track is where most of my favorite books have come from. It's also where I've had the most unexpected thoughts — the kind that arrive not because I was looking for them, but because I had been somewhere quiet long enough for them to find me.

---

Read without a purpose sometimes. Let the book go where it goes. The return is harder to measure than a system of notes, but it's real.
