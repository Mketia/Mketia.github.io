Most of the best writing I've read was written slowly. Not reluctantly, not procrastinatingly, but slowly by choice — with the writer taking time between drafts to let the material settle.

There is a pressure now, especially online, to produce quickly. Algorithms reward consistency. Inboxes fill faster than they empty. The writer who publishes once a week gets more followers than the writer who publishes once a quarter, regardless of quality.

I've spent a long time resisting this, and I want to say something about why.

## The first draft is not the thing

When I write a first draft, I'm doing something closer to excavation than construction. I'm finding out what I think by putting it down. Most of what I write in a first draft is wrong — or at least incomplete. The direction becomes clear only in retrospect, once the thing exists to be looked at.

This means that speed in the first draft isn't actually speed. It's just arriving at the starting line faster.

> The draft that feels complete is rarely the one you should publish. The one worth publishing usually requires a return — often several returns, over days or weeks.

The gap between drafts is where the work happens. I'll set something aside and come back to it to find that what I thought was a conclusion was actually a question in disguise. Or that the essay I thought was about one thing is really about something else entirely.

## What slow writing protects

Writing slowly protects a few things I care about.

**Honesty.** When I write quickly and publish immediately, I tend to write what I already believe, or what sounds good in the moment. The slower process creates distance from my own certainty — a chance to interrogate the premise, to ask whether I actually know what I think I know.

**The reader's time.** Someone will give their attention to what I write. That's not a small thing. If I owe them anything, I owe them care — the assurance that I sat with this material long enough to be confident it's worth passing on.

**The work itself.** Writing is a craft. Crafts are developed through practice and patience, not just output. Publishing slowly means I spend more time with each piece, which means I learn more from each piece.

## A practical note

I draft in a plain text editor and let things sit for at least a week before considering a piece finished. Often longer. I keep a folder called `archive/drafts` full of things that were almost something — essays that stalled, observations that didn't grow.

Occasionally I go back in there and find something worth finishing. More often I don't. But the act of writing them was not wasted. Every draft teaches you something, even the ones that go nowhere.

The goal is not speed or volume. The goal is to say something true.
