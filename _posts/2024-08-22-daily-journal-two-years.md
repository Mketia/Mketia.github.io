Two years ago I committed to writing something every morning before opening my email. The rule was simple: pen and paper, at least a page, no editing. It didn't matter what — the point was to sit down and move the pen.

Here is what I learned.

## The first twenty minutes are junk

Almost without exception, the first half of any morning entry is noise. Complaints about not sleeping well, a list of tasks masquerading as reflection, fragments of dreams I don't actually care about. The voice in the early pages is the voice of the not-quite-awake mind, circling rather than landing.

I used to try to fix this by starting with prompts. That helped a little, but the better solution was just accepting it. The junk is the warm-up. The real writing starts around page two.

## You think you know what you're anxious about

One of the most useful things about sustained journaling is the gap it reveals between what I think is bothering me and what's actually bothering me.

I might write three pages about a work project that isn't going well, and somewhere in page three, the real problem emerges: I'm not sure I believe in the project anymore. Or: I'm avoiding a conversation I need to have. The surface concern was real, but it was obscuring the thing underneath.

Journaling gives those underlying things room to surface. It won't happen in five minutes. It often won't happen in one session. But over weeks and months, patterns become visible.

## Writing about a problem is not solving it

This sounds obvious, but it took me a while to absorb. The journal is not a solution machine. It's a clarity machine — and clarity and resolution are different things.

Understanding clearly why something is hard does not make it less hard. I've written excellent, clear-eyed diagnoses of situations I then failed to change for months.

What journaling provides is a kind of distance. When I write something down, it moves slightly outside me. I can see it more objectively. That's genuinely useful. But it's the beginning of work, not the end.

## On consistency

I missed about forty days in two years. Some through travel, some through choice, a few through forgetting. I don't regret any of them. The practice is not broken by a missed day.

What matters is returning. Not guiltlessly — the guilt is part of caring — but without making the missed days into a reason to stop.

---

I still do this every morning. The notebooks stack up on a shelf, unlabeled. I rarely re-read them. The value is in the doing, not the archive. But occasionally something useful finds its way from those pages into the work, and that's enough.
